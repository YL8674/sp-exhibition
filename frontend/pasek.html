Anne Pasek

On Being Anxious About Digital Carbon Emissions

I was recently asked to discuss the ‘backstages’ of my research process. The prompt was a good one: there’s a lot of tangents, doubts, and tools that get hidden from the ‘frontstages’ of talks and publications—academics generally don’t like to show off their messes and mistakes. I, however, was increasingly keen to. I’ve lately begun to wonder about an emerging set of emotions and gestures that seem to be an inexorable part of working on the climate impacts of digital media. After all, we’re often stuck critiquing the very digital stages on which we are speaking, whether that’s Zoom, data centre archives, or the servers that host our words. It can make you feel like a cartoon character on a plank suspended over a cliff, sawing through the wood you’re standing on. The results can be both awkward and illuminating.

Let me start by painting a few scenes. In the first, I’m about to launch into a multi-day online workshop on information and communications technologies (ICT), art, and climate justice. The goal is to bring a group of technologists and media artists together to explore how the arts and tech sectors are currently imbricated—and might be reconfigured in future—as part of a wider decarbonization plan made for and by art workers. My job is to summarize and organize months of research from a winding set of empirical and interpretive fields. I have an extensive set of infographics and statistics to share, detailing the carbon footprint of everything from sending an email (as little as 4 grams (Richards, 2018)) to running the entire bitcoin network (up to 46.72 Mt/year—equivalent to the nation of Finland (Bitcoin Energy Consumption Index, n.d.)). The ICT sector’s climate emissions are, I will soon urge, an urgent problem. Effectively addressing them will mean disrupting the aesthetic and operational norms of some of the largest corporations on earth.
